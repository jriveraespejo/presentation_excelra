Step 1: Identify relevant factors

Two options:

  • A \(2^{k}\) full factorial design (CRFD) [1]:

    • Benefit: estimates the intercept, all \(7\) main effects, \(21\) two-factor interactions, \(7\) pure quadratic terms, and higher-order effects.
    • Drawback: requires \(2^{k} = 2^{7} = 128\) experimental runs.

  • A resolution III fractional factorial design (CRFF) [1]:

    • Benefit: requires only \(2^{k-p} = 2^{7-4} = 8\) experimental runs when using \(p=4\) generators.
    • Drawbacks: estimates only the intercept and the \(7\) main effects, has no \(\text{df}\), and main effects are aliased with two-factor interactions.

Because we are screening we choose the second option.

Three principles

  1. Effect Sparsity-Pareto S(P):

    around 80% of the variability is explained by 20% of the factors

  2. Hierarchical ordering H:

    main effects are more likely to be important than two-factor interactions, and this more important than three-factor interactions and so on.

  3. Heredity-Marginality H(M):

    usually interactions occur between factors where at least one of the two main effects are significant.

Step 3: Bayesian Optimization

Within this high-yield region, we implement Bayesian Optimization (BO) [2, 3].

Specifically:

  1. We use relevant initial training data:

    Three points from the steepest ascend procedure located within the high-yield region.

  2. We use a surrogate model to approximate the yield surface for a set of candidate data points:

    A Gaussian Process with Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel [3, 4].

  3. We use an acquisition function to determine the next point to evaluate in the search space:

    The Lower Confidence Bound (LCB) function.

  4. We define a stopping criterion to determine when to terminate the search:

    A central composite design [1] requires a total of \(12\) runs: \(4\) factorial points, \(4\) axial (face) points, and \(4\) center points.

    Our BO procedure will use only \(8\) runs.

Annex: Additional considerations

In a real-world setting, the identification of those \(7\) factors probably comes from experiments that seek to determine Sources of Variability. e.g.,

  • RSE (Random Statistical Error): Natural fluctuations in biological systems.
  • NSE (Non-Systematic Error): Potential issues with equipment or batch effects.

What happens when the problem grows?

  • Multiple Objectives: If we need high Yield but low Waste, we employ Desirability Functions to find the “Sweet Spot” that balances both.
  • Extrapolation:
    • Empirical models (RSM/BO) are dangerous outside their tested range.
    • If we must predict outside our data, we shift toward Theoretical/Mechanistic Models based on biological first principles.
